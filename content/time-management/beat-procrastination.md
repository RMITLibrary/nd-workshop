    <p class="lead">There are heaps of techniques that can help beat procrastination. Here are just a few — give them a try and see if any work for you. You might even like to combine two or more to create your own procrastination beating technique.</p>
	<!-- START Pomodoro -->
	<div class="nd-two-col nd-two-col-right margin-bottom-zero">

			<h2>Pomodoro technique</h2>
			<p>At the gym, many people use interval training to improve fitness. They exercise for a set amount of time, rest, and then start again.</p>
			<p>The Pomodoro technique works in a similar way. You study for a focused period, take a short break, and then return to your work.</p>
			<p>This strategy recognises that your brain needs regular breaks or you can feeling tired, distracted, or overwhelmed.</p>
		</div>
	</div>
	<div class="accordion margin-top-sm">
		<div class="accordion-item">
		<h3 class="accordion-header" id="head-how-it-works-7798">
		<button class="accordion-button collapsed" type="button" data-bs-toggle="collapse" data-bs-target="#body-how-it-works-3470" aria-expanded="false" aria-controls="body-how-it-works-3470">How it works</button>
		</h3>
			<div id="body-how-it-works-3470" class="accordion-collapse collapse" aria-labelledby="head-how-it-works-7798">
				<div class="accordion-body">
				<ol>
				<li>Remove distractions from your study area. Put your phone on silent.</li>
				<li>Set a timer for a focused study session. Many people use 25 minutes. If 25 minutes feels too difficult at first, try 10 minutes and build up gradually.
				<p>		Keeping study intervals reasonably short can help you look after your brain and body. Regular breaks give you time to rest, move, eat, hydrate, and reset before returning to your work.</li>
				<li>When the timer ends, take a 5–10 minute break to rest, scroll, stretch, get a snack, or chat with a friend.</li>
				<li>When the timer rings again, return to your study session and repeat the process.</li>
				<li>After about 4 study rounds, take a longer break. Go for a walk, eat something, or do an activity you enjoy before starting another session if you still need to study.</li>
				</ol>
				<p>Like many study strategies, the Pomodoro technique works differently for everyone, so it’s okay to experiment and find a rhythm that suits you.</p>
				</div>
			</div>
		</div>
	</div>

			<h2>Body doubling</h2>
			<p>The basic idea of body doubling is, while you work on a task, another person is nearby working on their own task. You’re not collaborating or working together — their presence simply helps you stay engaged and on track.</p>
			<p>For some people, having another person nearby can make tasks feel more manageable. It can create a sense of accountability, reduce feelings of overwhelm, and make it easier to begin tasks. The presence of another person can also be calming or reassuring.</p>
		</div>
	</div>

					<li>use online “study with me” videos (there are lots on YouTube)</li>

				Body doubling can also help with everyday tasks. For example, some people find it easier to make a difficult phone call, organise paperwork, or clean their room when another person is nearby.<br />

<!-- START five minute rule -->
	<div class="nd-two-col nd-two-col-right margin-bottom-zero margin-top-xl">

		<div class="margin-top-zero">
			<h2>5-minute rule</h2>
			<p>Do you ever feel like you could sit down and do some seriously productive work… but you just can’t seem to make a start? The 5-minute rule is designed to help you take that first step.</p>
		</div>
	</div>
	<div class="accordion margin-top-sm">
		<div class="accordion-item">
			<h3 class="accordion-header" id="head-how-it-works-9731">
			<button class="accordion-button collapsed" type="button" data-bs-toggle="collapse" data-bs-target="#body-how-it-works-2184" aria-expanded="false" aria-controls="body-how-it-works-2184">How it works</button>
			</h3>
			<div id="body-how-it-works-2184" class="accordion-collapse collapse" aria-labelledby="head-how-it-works-9731">
				<div class="accordion-body">
					<ul>
						<li>Select a task you are having trouble getting started on. Make sure it’s something small- don’t set yourself the goal of “finishing Assignment 2”, for example. Something achievable like “write one paragraph” is much better.</li>
						<li>Set a timer for 5-minutes. Before you start the timer, remove distractions from your study area.</li>
						<li>When the time starts, you start.</li>
						<li>Study for five minutes without stopping.</li>
					</ul>
					<p>	When the time’s up, you have two choices:</p>
					<ol>
						<li>Keep going</li>
						<li>Stop</li>
					</ol>
					<p>If you keep going, fantastic! If you decide to stop, congratulate yourself for getting started on something you find difficult. And you can always start again later!</p>
				</div>
			</div>
		</div>
	</div>
<!-- END five minute rule -->

			<h2>Create urgency</h2>
			<p>Some neurodivergent students find it easier to focus when there is a clear sense of urgency. If this sounds familiar, you could try creating your own deadlines to help keep yourself on track.</p>
			<p>Having a time limit can make tasks feel more immediate and easier to start. It can also help create momentum and make it easier to stay engaged with a task.</p>
			<p>This strategy works best when you begin before the last minute. Rather than relying on panic or stress, try breaking larger tasks into smaller steps and giving each step its own deadline.</p>
		</div>
	</div>

			<h2>Make it pleasant</h2>
			<p>A lot of the time, tasks we avoid are things that feel under stimulating, boring, mentally draining, emotionally taxing, or physically unpleasant.</p>
			<p>Maybe you hate cleaning the bathroom because it feels gross. Maybe writing an email to your lecturer asking for an extension feels emotionally exhausting. Or perhaps reading academic articles makes you yawn just thinking about it.</p>
			<p>One way to make tasks feel less daunting is to pair them with something you enjoy or find comforting.</p>
			<p>First, decide on the task you need to begin. Then think about the things that help you feel happy, calm, comfortable, motivated, or focused. Finally, see whether you can combine the two.</p>
		</div>
	</div>
	<div class="accordion margin-top-sm">
		<div class="accordion-item">
			<h3 class="accordion-header" id="head-how-it-works-12">
			<button class="accordion-button collapsed" type="button" data-bs-toggle="collapse" data-bs-target="#body-how-it-works-13" aria-expanded="false" aria-controls="body-how-it-works-13">How it works</button>
			</h3>
			<div id="body-how-it-works-13" class="accordion-collapse collapse" aria-labelledby="head-how-it-works-12">
				<div class="accordion-body">
					<p>For example:</p>
					<ul>
						<li>If you enjoy warm cafés but struggle with academic readings, challenge yourself to read one difficult article while sitting in a café you like.</li>
						<li>If comfortable lighting, music, or a particular type of tea helps you feel settled, try incorporating those things into your study environment.</li>
					</ul>
					<p>Making tasks more pleasant is not just about rewards or treats. It is also about understanding yourself and recognising the environments, routines, and sensory experiences that help you feel comfortable and able to focus.</p>
					<p>Want to take it a step further? Try <em>temptation bundling</em>. The idea is simple: you “bundle” something you like with something you tend to avoid.<br>
					For example:</p>
					<ul>
						<li>only listening to your favourite podcast while cleaning the house</li>
						<li>saving your favourite snack for study sessions </li>
						<li>treating yourself to a special breakfast while organising your week</li>
						<li>using your favourite colourful markers and sticky notes while reading difficult articles </li>
						<li>listening to music you enjoy while doing repetitive admin tasks.</li>
					</ul>
					<p>Temptation bundling can help make difficult tasks feel less intimidating and give you something positive to look forward to while you work.</p>
				</div>
			</div>

			<p>Some people enjoy visually tracking their progress. Crossing things off a list, filling in habit trackers, or watching progress bars move can create a sense of momentum and accomplishment.</p>
			<p>If this sounds like you, then it might help to gamify your tasks. Gamifying means turning a task into a challenge, or achievement-based activity.</p>
