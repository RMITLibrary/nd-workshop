	<h2>Strategies to try</h2>
	<ul>
		<li><strong>5-Minute rule</strong> — just start for 5 minutes; momentum carries you</li>
		<li><strong>Make it pleasant</strong> — favourite music, good snack, comfortable spot</li>
		<li><strong>Create urgency</strong> — set artificial deadlines, tell a friend, use a timer</li>
		<li><strong>Body doubling</strong> — work alongside someone; try ND study sessions (Tue, Wed &amp; Thu, 12–4pm) or Focusmate</li>
		<li><strong>Gamify it</strong> — apps like Habitica, or reward yourself after completing tasks</li>
	</ul>
	<h2>Body doubling</h2>
	<p><strong>[Image here]</strong></p>
	<p>
	Body doubling is a strategy often used by people with ADHD, but many people find it helpful to stay focused and get started on tasks. The basic idea is, while you work on a task, another person is nearby working on their own task. You’re not collaborating or working together — their presence simply helps you stay engaged and on track.
	</p>
	<p>
	For some people, having another person nearby can make tasks feel more manageable. It can create a sense of accountability, reduce feelings of overwhelm, and make it easier to begin tasks that are difficult or uninteresting. The presence of another person can also be calming or reassuring.
	</p>
	<p>Body doubling can be done in different ways. You might:</p>
	<ul>
		<li>study in the same room as a friend</li>
		<li>join a virtual study session</li>
		<li>use online “study with me” videos (there are lots on YouTube)</li>
		<li>work in a public space such as a library or café</li>
	</ul>
	<p>
	Body doubling can also help with everyday tasks. For example, some people find it easier to make a difficult phone call, organise paperwork, or clean their room when another person is nearby.
	</p>
	<p>
	Body doubling can be combined with other strategies, such as using a Pomodoro timer, turning off phone notifications, or reducing distractions in your study space.
	</p>
