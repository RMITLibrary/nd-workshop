            <!-- START info-pomodoro -->
			<div id="info-pomodoro" class="info-technique">
				<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> The pomodoro technique</h3>
				<ul>
				<li>Remove distractions from your study area.</li>
				<li>Set yourself one small goal for the each interval. For example, read one article, write one paragraph, etc.</li>
				<li>Set a timer for 25 minutes. If this is too long for you, you can try a short interval and slowly build up.</li>
				<li>Study without stopping until the timer rings.</li>
				<li>Set the time for a shorter period &mdash; such as 5 or 10 minutes. Take a break, let your mind rest.</li>
				<li>Repeat the process 3 or 4 times, then take a longer break. Get some lunch, go for a walk, do something you enjoy.</li>
				<li>If you have more study to do, then do another round of pomodoro.</li>
				</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-pomodoro -->
			<!-- START info-body-doubling -->
			<div id="info-body-doubling" class="info-technique">
				<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> Body doubling</h3>
				<ul>
				<li>Try studying in the same room as a friend, with both of you working on your own tasks.</li>
				<li>You can also try body doubling in a public space like a library or a cafe.</li>
				<li>You try this technique in virtual study sessions.</li>
				<li>There are many 'study with me' videos on YouTube that can be used for body doubling.</li>
				</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-body-doubling -->
			<!-- START info-prioritisation -->
			<div id="info-prioritisation" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> Prioritisation</h3>
			<p>We have adapted the Eisenhow Matrix into a matrix called the Student time matrix. It can help you to decide which tasks to:</p>
			<ul>
			<li><strong>Do now</strong> because they are urgent and important.</li>
			<li><strong>Plan</strong> for because they are important but not urgent.</li>
			<li><strong>Get done quickly</strong> because they are urgent but not important.</li>
			<li><strong>Let go</strong> of because they are neither urgent nor important.</li>
			</ul>
			<p>Also, Microsoft Planner can help you to:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Group your projects and put sub tasks under each project.</li>
			<li>Track projects over time.</li>
			<li>Mark tasks as not yet started, in progress and complete.</li>
			<li>Add due dates for tasks.</li>
			<li>Give priority labels to tasks such as, 'do now', 'plan now, do later' and 'get done quickly'.</li>
			</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-prioritisation -->
			<!-- START info-create-urgency -->
			<div id="info-create-urgency" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> Creating urgency</h3>
			<p>If you are the type of person who needs a deadline to help you get started, you can try setting your own deadlines rather than waiting for assessment due dates. To do this:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Break you assessments down into smaller tasks. For example, find literature, do background reading, write first draft.</li>
			<li>Then, assign a due date to each of these tasks and put them into your calendar.</li>
			</ul>
			<p>This can help you to recreate the sense of urgency you feel when you have a looming submission date.</p>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-create-urgency -->
			<!-- START info-5-minute-rule -->
			<div id="info-5-minute-rule" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> The 5-minute rule</h3>
			<ul>
			<li>Select a task you are having trouble getting started on. Make sure it’s something small.</li>
			<li>Remove distractions from your study area.</li>
			<li>Set a timer for 5-minutes.</li>
			<li>Study without stopping for 5 minutes.</li>
			<li>When the 5 minutes are up, decide whether to keep studying or take a break.</li>
			<li>If you decide to keep studying, you might like to set your timer for 10 minutes, then 15, then 20...</li>
			</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-5-minute-rule -->
			<!-- START info-make-it-pleasant -->
			<div id="info-make-it-pleasant" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> The make it pleasant technique</h3>
			<p>Pair tasks that you have been avoiding or that you don't enjoy very much with things that you do enjoy. For example:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Take your laptop to a park to read an academic articles.</li>
			<li>Eat your favourite snack while you write an essay draft.</li>
			</ul>
			<p>If you want to try a more extreme version of this, try temptation bundling. For this, you only do something you enjoy when you are doing something that you are avoiding or don't like. Stuff like:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Planning your calendar while having eating chocolate cake.</li>
			<li>Listening to lecture recordings while working out at the gym.</li>
			</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-make-it-pleasant -->
			<!-- START info-gamify-it -->
			<div id="info-gamify-it" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> The gamify it technique</h3>
			<p>If you the sort of person who loves a challenge, then gamifying your studies could work for you. Here are some things you could try:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Progress bar: Create a visual progress bar and colour in sections as you complete study tasks. For example, divide the bar into ten sections and colour in one section each time you complete a task such as reading an article, watching a lecture or writing a paragraph.</li>
			<li>Race the clock: Challenge yourself to see how much you can complete in a short amount of time. For example, set a timer for 10 minutes and see if you can read two pages, summarise a lecture slide or write 100 words before the timer ends.</li>
			<li>Boss battles: Turn difficult or overwhelming tasks into "boss battles" and break them into smaller stages. For example, instead of "write essay", create mini battles such as finding sources, writing an introduction and completing a first draft.</li>
			<li>Co-op study: Study alongside another person and complete tasks together while working separately. For example, both people decide on a goal, work for 20 minutes and then check in on what they completed.</li>
			<li>Reward unlocks: Choose a small reward that is unlocked after completing a study task. For example, after finishing a reading you might unlock a coffee, 10 minutes on social media, a snack or an episode of a favourite show.</li>
			</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-gamify-it -->
			<!-- START info-break-down-assessments -->
			<div id="info-break-down-assessments" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> Breaking down assessments</h3>
			<p>Breaking your tasks down into small chunks can make things seem less daunting. It can also help you to priorities particular tasks.</p>
			<p>Assignment Planner can help you to:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Break an assessment into steps, such as reading assessment instructions, researching, writing a first draft, etc.</li>
			<li>Understand what is required for each step.</li>
			<li>Create due dates for different tasks.</li>
			</ul>
			<p>Goblin Tools Magic ToDo is another handy tool. It can:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Create step-by-step checklists for broad tasks such as 'study for an exam' or 'complete assessment 1'. You can easily delete the steps you don't need.</li>
			<li>Estimate the time different steps will take to complete.</li>
			<li>Move tasks up and down the list to suit your priorities.</li>
			</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-break-down-assessments -->
			<!-- START info-schedule-timeblock -->
			<div id="info-schedule-timeblock" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> Scheduling and timeblocking</h3>
			<ul>
			<li>You can use any online calendar, including Outlook.</li>
			<li>Start by adding fixed commitments such as classes and work shifts. You can set these to repeat each week.</li>
			<li>Schedule flixible study time to get your course work done.</li>
			<li>Be specific with the labels you use in your calendar. For example, "1st draft for Assessment 2".</li>
			<li>Schedule smaller, clearly defined tasks (1-2 hours), rather than long blocks of study.</li>
			<li>Add 10 to 15-minute buffers around tasks, rather than scheduling back-to-back tasks.</li>
			<li>Plan breaks into your calendar.</li>
			<li>Use colour-coding to organise your week visually.</li>
			<li>Set up reminders. Adjust the timing of reminders to suit your preferences.</li>
			</ul>
			</div>
			<!-- END info-schedule-timeblock -->
			<!-- START info-externalising -->
			<div id="info-externalising" class="info-technique">
			<h3><div class="little-heading">Things to remember about</div> Externalising</h3>
			<p>Getting thoughts and taks out of your head and onto an app or piece of paper can help to free up your brain for other information.</p>
			<p>Sticky notes are great for this. They are great for:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Task reminders.</li>
			<li>Short term priorities.</li>
			<li>Giving you a break from digital tools.</li>
			</ul>
			<p>And here are some tips for using them:</p>
			<ul>
			<li>Don't use too many. Seeing dozens of sticky notes around your study space can be overwhelming.</li>
			<li>Only put one important task on each sticky note.</li>
			<li>Remove notes as you complete them.</li>
			</ul>
			</div>
